Bipolar and the Way
it Affects Your Life

Written By Palvisha Shoaib

Maya Talpur was 17 years old and lived in a tiny village in Pakistan where talking about mental health was
taboo when she started struggling. By the time she was finally diagnosed with bipolar disorder, she was 22
and it was affecting her education, work and relationships.

“I get mad insomnia, I can’t sleep because I have so much energy,” said Talpur, who is a 30-year-old artist
living in Austin. “I’m super high-powered. It’s like someone putting extra batteries in you and you’re running on full power.”

Talpur is a painter who creates abstract art that explains her thoughts, experiences and feelings on a canvas.
Her paintings are a mix of acrylics on a blank canvas sheet or are textured.

Talpur said that she always carries her paints and a notebook with her, and she has a habit of fixing her artwork
every time she sees them hanging on her friends’ walls. She immediately pulls them down, and starts to find and fix
that missing detail in that painting.

“At that time, mania was a blessing and a curse to me. I was an insomniac and felt like I had a superpower and was
invincible,” Talpur said. “I would spend my salary on all the things I didn’t need and then used to cry in the end with
a Louis Vuitton scarf around my neck during my depressive episode.”

and it enables them to work more than normal people. That is why they don’t go to the psychiatrist
or seek treatment because they don’t want their mania to heal.

“Some people get the wrong perception of mania and think that mania is good, because they have more
energy than others, and they don’t want that to be taken away,” Werchan said.

According to Talpur, bipolar disorder causes a lot of unpredictability. She can feel neutral today,
and tomorrow she could go into her depressive or manic episode. She doesn’t know what uncontrollable
effect of the outside world could trigger her mania or depression.

“I don’t have a neutral functional daily life every day, and that’s what affects the most in my daily chores,”
Talpur said.
“If I’m feeling neutral, and then suddenly I’m depressed, I have to cancel all the plans I made when I was
OK because I wouldn’t have the energy to carry out the plans if I’m depressed.”

Talpur said she creates a lot of paintings at once during a manic phase and abandons them when she is depressed.
All of her art is under construction. She either gets back to them after some time, or her art
and feelings stay hidden between her canvas stacks.
